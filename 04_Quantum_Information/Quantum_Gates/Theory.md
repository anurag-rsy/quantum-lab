Quantum gates are the fundamental building blocks of quantum circuits, used to manipulate information in quantum computers. In classical computers, transistors process information in the form of 0s and 1s; similarly, quantum gates manipulate quantum information. However, quantum gates process fluid and complex data. They accept qubits that exist in a state of superposition. Furthermore, while classical logic operations are often irreversible—meaning you cannot always determine the original input from the output—quantum gates are entirely reversible.Quantum gates are broadly categorized into two types: single-qubit gates and multi-qubit gates. Examples of single-qubit gates include the Pauli-X gate and the Hadamard gate. The Pauli-X gate flips a single qubit, acting similarly to a classical NOT gate. The Hadamard gate puts qubits into a state of superposition. A prominent example of a multi-qubit gate is the CNOT (Controlled-NOT) gate. This gate can place qubits into entangled states, flipping the target qubit only if the control qubit is in a specific state.
